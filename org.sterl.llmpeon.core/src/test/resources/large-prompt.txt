THE GERMAN BUNDESTAG: HISTORY, STRUCTURE, AND POLITICAL SIGNIFICANCE

================================================================================
PROLOGUE: PARLIAMENT AS THE HEART OF DEMOCRACY
================================================================================

The German Bundestag is the central legislative organ of the Federal Republic of
Germany and the only institution at the federal level directly elected by the
German people. It is often referred to as "Das Herz der Demokratie" — the heart
of democracy — a designation that reflects not only its constitutional function
but also the hard-won democratic culture that Germany had to rebuild from near
total destruction after 1945. To understand the Bundestag is to understand the
entire arc of modern German political history: the failure of early democratic
experiments, the catastrophe of National Socialism, the long careful
reconstruction of parliamentary governance in the postwar decades, the drama of
reunification, and the ongoing challenges of a mature democracy navigating
globalization, populism, and institutional reform.

The Bundestag sits in the Reichstag building in Berlin, a structure whose walls
contain more political history than almost any other building in Europe. That
fact alone is worth dwelling on. The same building that housed the imperial
Reichstag of the Wilhelmine era, that burned in 1933 under circumstances still
disputed by historians, that stood gutted and symbolically irrelevant throughout
the Cold War, was restored by architect Norman Foster and reopened in 1999 as
the home of a reunified Germany's parliament. The glass dome on top, open to the
public, was a deliberate architectural statement: citizens looking down on their
representatives at work. Transparency as built form.

================================================================================
PART ONE: HISTORICAL ROOTS — BEFORE THE BUNDESTAG
================================================================================

The Bundestag did not emerge from nothing. It is the latest — and strongest —
chapter in a long and turbulent history of German parliamentary institutions.

The Frankfurt Parliament of 1848/1849 (Frankfurter Nationalversammlung) was
Germany's first serious attempt at a national democratic parliament. It convened
in St. Paul's Church (Paulskirche) in Frankfurt and spent almost a year debating
a liberal constitution for a unified Germany. It failed. The Prussian king
Friedrich Wilhelm IV refused the imperial crown offered by the parliament,
dismissing it as coming from "the gutter." The revolution collapsed. But the
Frankfurt Parliament established something important: the idea that a German
nation-state could and should be governed through parliamentary representation.
The Paulskirche tradition became a reference point invoked repeatedly in later
German democratic politics, most explicitly when the West German Bundestag held
a commemorative session there in 1948, symbolically connecting the new
democratic order to its pre-authoritarian roots.

The North German Confederation (1867) and then the German Empire founded in 1871
introduced the Reichstag — an elected parliament for the unified German state.
It was elected by universal and equal male suffrage, which was genuinely
progressive for its time. However, the Reichstag of the Kaiserreich operated
under severe constitutional constraints. The Chancellor was responsible to the
Kaiser, not to the Reichstag. The parliament could debate and could block
budgets, but it could not appoint or remove governments. Real power lay with the
executive, particularly the Chancellor and the Emperor. The Reichstag was a
parliament with no parliamentary government — a fundamental structural weakness
that would have lasting consequences.

The First World War shattered the imperial order. In October 1918, with military
defeat imminent, Germany hastily introduced parliamentary reform — the October
Reforms transformed the constitutional structure so that the Chancellor became
dependent on Reichstag confidence. But this came too late, under the worst
possible circumstances. When the Kaiser abdicated and the Republic was proclaimed
in November 1918, the new democratic order inherited the blame for military
defeat, the humiliation of Versailles, and the economic chaos of the early 1920s.

The Weimar Republic (1919–1933) was Germany's first genuine parliamentary
democracy. The Weimar Constitution of 1919 was, on paper, one of the most
advanced democratic constitutions in the world. It established a republic with a
strong parliament, proportional representation, universal suffrage (including
women for the first time), and a directly elected president with significant
emergency powers under Article 48. That last provision became one of the
republic's fatal weaknesses. The proportional representation system produced a
highly fragmented parliament with many parties, making stable coalition
government difficult. The presidential emergency powers were progressively
expanded and abused, particularly between 1930 and 1933, when governments ruled
increasingly by decree rather than parliamentary majority.

The Weimar Republic faced hyperinflation in 1923, a brief period of stabilization
from 1924 to 1929, then the catastrophic impact of the Great Depression from 1929
onward. Mass unemployment, political radicalization, and street violence between
paramilitaries of the left and right created conditions of permanent crisis. The
National Socialist German Workers' Party (NSDAP) under Adolf Hitler exploited
this crisis systematically. In the July 1932 elections the NSDAP became the
largest party in the Reichstag with 37.4 percent of the vote. On January 30,
1933, Hitler was appointed Chancellor. The Enabling Act (Ermächtigungsgesetz) of
March 23, 1933, passed with the required two-thirds majority after the Communist
deputies had been arrested and the Social Democrats cast the only votes against,
transferred full legislative power to the Nazi government. The Reichstag voted
itself out of existence as a meaningful institution. It continued to meet for
ceremonial purposes — most infamously to applaud Hitler's announcements of war —
but as a legislature it was finished.

The Reichstag building itself burned on February 27, 1933. The Nazis blamed
the Communist Party, arrested thousands, and used the fire as pretext to suspend
civil liberties under the Reichstag Fire Decree. Historians continue to debate
the precise circumstances. What is certain is that the burning of the building
and the destruction of parliamentary democracy occurred in the same historical
moment, and that the Nazis understood the propaganda value of that coincidence.

================================================================================
PART TWO: FOUNDING THE BUNDESTAG — THE FEDERAL REPUBLIC, 1949
================================================================================

When the Second World War ended in May 1945 with Germany's unconditional
surrender, the country was divided into four occupation zones controlled by the
United States, Britain, France, and the Soviet Union. The immediate postwar years
were dominated by denazification efforts (inconsistently applied), the
reconstruction of civil society, and the deepening of the emerging Cold War
divide between the Western Allies and the Soviet Union.

The founding of the Federal Republic of Germany was a deliberate response to the
failures of Weimar. The Parliamentary Council (Parlamentarischer Rat), composed
of representatives from the West German Länder, met in Bonn from September 1948
to May 1949. Its president was Konrad Adenauer. Its task was to draft a Basic Law
(Grundgesetz) — deliberately not called a "constitution" to signal its provisional
character, since Germany remained divided. The Basic Law came into force on
May 23, 1949.

The Basic Law incorporated explicit lessons from Weimar's failure. The President
was made a largely ceremonial figure, stripped of the emergency powers that had
enabled authoritarian rule under Article 48. The Chancellor became the center of
executive power and could be removed only through a "constructive vote of no
confidence" (konstruktives Misstrauensvotum) — meaning the Bundestag could only
remove a Chancellor by simultaneously electing a replacement. This was designed
to prevent the negative parliamentary majorities that had destabilized Weimar
governments. Parties that did not receive at least five percent of the national
vote or win at least three direct constituency seats were barred from the
Bundestag — the five-percent threshold (Fünfprozenthürde) designed to prevent
the extreme parliamentary fragmentation that had paralyzed Weimar. The
Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) was given strong powers to
protect the constitution, including the ability to ban anti-constitutional
parties. Fundamental rights were made directly legally binding, not merely
programmatic declarations.

The first Bundestag election took place on August 14, 1949. Eleven parties and
two independent candidates won seats. The CDU/CSU won 31 percent, the SPD 29.2
percent, and the FDP 11.9 percent. On September 7, 1949, the Bundestag held its
first constitutive session in Bonn. On September 15, Konrad Adenauer was elected
the first Federal Chancellor with a majority of exactly one vote — reportedly
his own.

The choice of Bonn as the seat of government was itself politically significant.
Bonn was a small, provincial Rhineland city, far from the power centers of
Prussian history. Its selection signaled the provisional nature of the
arrangement and avoided the symbolic weight of cities like Frankfurt, Munich, or
Hamburg. When critics called Bonn the "Bundeshauptdorf" (federal capital village),
the provisional-capital status was implicitly acknowledged.

================================================================================
PART THREE: THE ADENAUER ERA AND THE CONSOLIDATION OF DEMOCRACY (1949–1963)
================================================================================

Konrad Adenauer's fourteen years as Chancellor (1949–1963) shaped the Federal
Republic's political culture in fundamental ways. His era is characterized in
political science as "Kanzlerdemokratie" — Chancellor democracy — a system in
which the Chancellor so dominated the political landscape that the Bundestag's
role was somewhat overshadowed by executive authority, though never undermined in
constitutional terms.

Adenauer anchored West Germany firmly in the Western alliance, oversaw West
German rearmament and NATO membership in 1955, and negotiated the restoration of
full sovereignty in the same year through the Paris Treaties. He pursued European
integration — the Treaties of Rome (1957) creating the European Economic
Community were signed during his tenure. His domestic economic policy, guided by
Economics Minister Ludwig Erhard and the concept of the "social market economy"
(Soziale Marktwirtschaft), presided over the Wirtschaftswunder — the economic
miracle that transformed a bombed-out country into one of the world's leading
economies within a decade.

The 1950s saw the Bundestag party system consolidate rapidly. The CDU/CSU and
SPD absorbed or displaced most smaller parties. By 1957, when Adenauer won an
absolute majority (the only time in Bundestag history a single party has achieved
this), the parliament had effectively become a three-party system of CDU/CSU, SPD,
and FDP.

The Bundestag in this era ratified critical foreign policy decisions but was not
yet the kind of active, adversarial legislature it would become. The opposition
SPD was in a structurally weak position: associated in voters' minds with Weimar
and socialist ideology, it struggled to break through until its Bad Godesberg
Congress of 1959, where it formally abandoned Marxist economic doctrine and
repositioned itself as a modern social democratic party.

Adenauer's methods were often authoritarian in temperament, if not in law. The
Spiegel Affair of 1962 became a pivotal moment. When the news magazine Der
Spiegel published an article about NATO exercises that the government deemed
a security breach, the Defense Ministry under Franz Josef Strauß orchestrated
an illegal raid on the Spiegel's offices and the arrest of several editors. The
public and Bundestag reaction was fierce. The FDP threatened to leave the
coalition unless Strauß was removed. He was. The affair demonstrated that the
Bundestag and civil society could check executive overreach even during the
Adenauer era, and it established a tradition of press freedom defense that
remains central to German political culture.

================================================================================
PART FOUR: GRAND COALITION, STUDENT MOVEMENT, AND THE SOCIAL-LIBERAL ERA (1966–1982)
================================================================================

Adenauer's successor Ludwig Erhard (1963–1966) was more economist than
politician and struggled to manage the coalition dynamics. His government fell
over a budget dispute. The result was Germany's first Grand Coalition
(Große Koalition), with Kurt Georg Kiesinger (CDU) as Chancellor and Willy
Brandt (SPD) as Foreign Minister, from 1966 to 1969.

The Grand Coalition was a governing arrangement that left only the FDP as formal
parliamentary opposition, covering barely ten percent of seats. This democratic
deficit — the near-total absence of effective parliamentary opposition — was
one of the factors that energized the extra-parliamentary opposition (APO,
Außerparlamentarische Opposition), centered on the student movement of 1967/68.
The assassination attempt on student leader Rudi Dutschke in April 1968 triggered
mass protests across West Germany. The APO demanded the Grand Coalition's
Emergency Laws (Notstandsgesetze) be rejected as a step toward
re-authoritarianism. The laws passed anyway, but the 1968 movement permanently
transformed West German political culture, introducing a generation of activists
who would later shape the SPD, the Greens, and German civil society.

The 1969 Bundestag election produced a narrow SPD-FDP majority. Willy Brandt
became Chancellor, the first Social Democrat to hold that office in the Federal
Republic. His era (1969–1974) is most associated with Ostpolitik — a radical
reorientation of West Germany's relationship with the East. Brandt normalized
relations with East Germany (accepting its existence without recognizing it as a
foreign state), signed the Warsaw Treaty acknowledging the postwar Polish border,
and in one of the most iconic moments of 20th-century diplomacy, knelt silently
at the Warsaw Ghetto memorial in December 1970. He won the Nobel Peace Prize in
1971. In the Bundestag, his Ostpolitik was fiercely contested. The CDU/CSU
opposition attempted a constructive vote of no confidence in April 1972 and
came within two votes of succeeding — the closest the mechanism has ever come
to removing a Chancellor. Brandt survived and called new elections in November
1972, winning the SPD's best-ever result of 45.8 percent.

Brandt resigned in 1974 after one of his personal aides, Günter Guillaume, was
revealed to be an East German spy. Helmut Schmidt (SPD) succeeded him and led
the country from 1974 to 1982. Schmidt was a pragmatic manager who steered
Germany through the oil crisis, the global economic turbulence of the late 1970s,
and the terrorism of the Red Army Faction (RAF). His most controversial decision
was supporting NATO's dual-track decision of 1979, which proposed deploying new
American medium-range missiles in West Germany unless arms control negotiations
with the Soviets succeeded. This decision broke the SPD coalition apart. In 1982,
the FDP switched coalition partners. Helmut Kohl became Chancellor through a
constructive vote of no confidence — the only successful such vote in German
history.

================================================================================
PART FIVE: THE KOHL ERA, REUNIFICATION, AND THE MOVE TO BERLIN (1982–1998)
================================================================================

Helmut Kohl's sixteen years as Chancellor (1982–1998) span the final decade of
the Cold War and the dramatic events of 1989/1990. For much of the 1980s Kohl was
domestically unremarkable, maintaining the center-right coalition, pursuing
European integration alongside French President Mitterrand, and handling the
controversies around stationing American Pershing II missiles on German soil amid
mass protests.

The fall of the Berlin Wall on November 9, 1989 changed everything. The Monday
demonstrations in Leipzig and other East German cities, the collapse of the SED
regime's authority, and the accidental opening of the Wall transformed the
political situation within weeks. Kohl moved faster than anyone — including the
Western Allies and his own coalition — toward reunification. His Ten-Point Plan
for German Unity, announced before the Bundestag on November 28, 1989 without
prior consultation with allies, set the pace. By October 3, 1990, German
reunification was complete. The East German Volkskammer, in its only democratically
elected session (March 1990), voted to accede to the Federal Republic under
Article 23 of the Basic Law, the fastest and most constitutionally conservative
route.

Reunification reshaped the Bundestag. The new eastern Länder brought not only
new voters and new representatives but new political dynamics. The PDS (Party of
Democratic Socialism), successor to the East German SED, entered the Bundestag
as a significant force in eastern constituencies, representing voters whose
experiences under and after communism diverged sharply from the western majority.
The five-percent threshold created tensions: in the 1990 all-German election,
separate calculation zones were used to ensure eastern parties could enter
parliament despite not meeting the threshold nationally.

In 1991 the Bundestag voted narrowly (338 to 320) to move the seat of government
from Bonn back to Berlin. It was a fiercely contested decision. Opponents argued
that Bonn represented a successful democratic culture and that Berlin carried
too much historical weight — Prussian militarism, National Socialism, the Cold
War division. Supporters argued that the symbolic and practical case for the
capital of a unified Germany was unanswerable. The move happened in 1999. The
Reichstag building, restored by Norman Foster with its signature glass dome, held
its first plenary session of the Berlin Bundestag on September 7, 1999 — exactly
fifty years after the Bundestag's founding session in Bonn.

================================================================================
PART SIX: THE SCHRÖDER ERA AND THE AGENDA 2010 CONTROVERSY (1998–2005)
================================================================================

The 1998 election ended sixteen years of Kohl government. Gerhard Schröder (SPD)
formed a coalition with the Greens, the first Red-Green government at the federal
level. It was a generational shift: the 1968 movement's political heirs were
now in government. The Greens' Joschka Fischer became Foreign Minister, navigating
the contradiction of a pacifist party in government when Germany participated in
its first combat military operation since 1945 — the NATO air campaign against
Serbia over Kosovo in 1999.

Schröder's government presided over two defining decisions. The first was the
rejection of U.S. plans to invade Iraq in 2003. Schröder campaigned on explicit
opposition to the war and won re-election in 2002 partly on that basis. The
decision strained German-American relations severely but enjoyed broad public
support domestically and positioned Germany as a counterweight to American
unilateralism alongside France and Russia — a combination that alarmed many
Eastern European NATO allies.

The second defining act was Agenda 2010, announced in March 2003. This package
of labor market, welfare, and unemployment insurance reforms (including the
controversial Hartz IV system) was the most radical restructuring of Germany's
social state since the postwar settlement. It reduced long-term unemployment
benefits, increased pressure on unemployed workers to accept available jobs, and
cut public expenditure. The reforms provoked mass protests, shattered the SPD's
traditional working-class base, and ultimately led to the formation of the
Left Party (Die Linke) when former SPD Chairman Oskar Lafontaine united SPD
dissidents with the eastern PDS. The Bundestag passed the reforms, but the
political cost was enormous.

Schröder called a confidence vote he deliberately lost in 2005 to force early
elections — a constitutionally questionable maneuver that the Federal
Constitutional Court permitted — and the resulting election produced a
near-draw between CDU/CSU and SPD that led to Germany's second Grand Coalition.

================================================================================
PART SEVEN: THE MERKEL ERA (2005–2021)
================================================================================

Angela Merkel became Chancellor in November 2005, the first woman and the first
person from the former East Germany to hold the office. Her sixteen years are the
dominant chapter of 21st-century German politics.

Merkel's political style was pragmatic and deliberately unideological. She moved
the CDU toward the center on multiple issues — abandoning nuclear power after
Fukushima in 2011, introducing a statutory minimum wage, supporting marriage
equality. Critics called this "asymmetric demobilization" — occupying the center
ground to deprive the SPD of its issues. Supporters called it responsive
governance. The Bundestag under Merkel passed significant legislation on all
these fronts.

The Euro crisis from 2010 onward placed Germany at the center of European
financial governance. Merkel and Finance Minister Wolfgang Schäuble led German
policy toward the so-called "TINA" (There Is No Alternative) approach of austerity
conditionality for bailout recipients. German insistence on strict fiscal rules
was deeply controversial within Europe, particularly in Greece, but had strong
domestic support. The Bundestag voted repeatedly on European Stability Mechanism
measures — debates that were among the most substantive and consequential
parliamentary debates of the Merkel era.

The 2015 refugee crisis was the most politically consequential event of Merkel's
chancellorship. When Hungary closed its borders and thousands of refugees were
stranded, Merkel's government announced that Germany would process their asylum
claims regardless of the Dublin Regulation. Over a million people arrived in
Germany in 2015. Merkel's phrase "Wir schaffen das" (We can do this) became
either a symbol of humane leadership or political recklessness depending on
perspective. The political fallout was severe. The AfD (Alternative für
Deutschland), founded in 2013 as a Eurosceptic protest party, transformed into
a hard-right nationalist party capitalizing on refugee-related anxieties.

PART EIGHT: STRUCTURE AND FUNCTIONS OF THE BUNDESTAG
================================================================================

The Bundestag is currently composed of 630 members elected every four years under
a personalized proportional representation system. Each voter has two votes:
a first vote (Erststimme) for a direct constituency candidate in one of 299
constituencies, and a second vote (Zweitstimme) for a party list in their
federal state. The second vote determines the proportional distribution of seats.
Direct constituency winners always enter the Bundestag, but additional seats
(Überhangmandate and Ausgleichsmandate) have historically been added to restore
proportionality, leading to very large parliaments — the 20th Bundestag (2021)
had 736 members, the largest in German history. The 2023 electoral reform capped
the size at 630 seats.

The Bundestag's core constitutional functions are:

Legislation: The Bundestag is the primary legislative organ of the Federal
Republic. Laws require a simple majority of votes cast, though constitutional
amendments require a two-thirds majority of the total membership. The Federal
Council (Bundesrat), representing the state governments, has limited veto power
over legislation affecting state interests. Most legislation is initiated by the
Federal Government rather than by Bundestag members themselves, a structural
feature common to parliamentary systems.

Budget Authority: The Bundestag approves the federal budget and all supplementary
budgets. Germany's constitutionalization of the "debt brake" (Schuldenbremse) in
2009 — limiting structural federal deficits to 0.35 percent of GDP — placed a
constitutional constraint on the Bundestag's budgetary discretion that became
highly controversial during the COVID-19 pandemic and subsequent energy crisis.
The Federal Constitutional Court's 2023 ruling that emergency reclassifications
of pandemic funds for climate investment were unconstitutional struck down a
60-billion-euro package and brought down the Scholz coalition.

Chancellor Election: Only the Bundestag elects the Federal Chancellor, by an
absolute majority on the first ballot. The President nominates a candidate, but
if that candidate fails, the Bundestag can elect its own choice. If no candidate
wins an absolute majority in subsequent rounds, the President can either appoint
a Chancellor with a relative majority or dissolve the Bundestag.

Government Control: The Bundestag exercises oversight through question time
(Fragestunde), major interpellations (Große Anfragen), minor interpellations
(Kleine Anfragen), parliamentary investigation committees (Untersuchungsausschüsse),
and the Parliamentary Commissioner for the Armed Forces (Wehrbeauftragter).
Committee work — in 26 standing committees corresponding roughly to government
ministries — is where the substantive legislative work occurs, largely out of
public view.

Armed Forces Deployment: A distinctive feature of the Bundestag is its authority
over Bundeswehr deployments abroad. Each deployment requires Bundestag approval.
This parliamentary army (Parlamentsheer) concept was built into the constitutional
structure to prevent executive warmaking of the kind that had characterized the
imperial and Nazi eras.

PART NINE: THE PARTY SYSTEM — EVOLUTION AND FRAGMENTATION
================================================================================

The West German party system from 1961 to 1983 was famously stable: CDU/CSU,
SPD, FDP, and nothing else. The entry of the Greens in 1983 — the first new party
to enter the Bundestag in thirty years — marked the beginning of a longer process
of party system fragmentation.

The Greens emerged from the peace movement, the anti-nuclear movement, and the
feminist movement of the late 1970s. They entered the Bundestag with their famous
statement that they were "the alternative to the established parties" and their
refusal of ties and jackets. Over time they professionalized, moderated, split
(Realos vs. Fundis), and eventually became a governing party. Their 2021 result
of 14.8 percent under Annalena Baerbock, who came close to becoming Chancellor,
represented their peak federal influence.

The Left Party (Die Linke) entered the Bundestag in 2005, combining the eastern
PDS with western SPD dissidents. It consistently represented the interests of
eastern Germans skeptical of reunification's economic outcomes and of western
leftists dissatisfied with SPD centrism. In the 2021 election it barely cleared
the five-percent threshold and in 2024 it fell below it, its future uncertain.

The AfD entered the Bundestag in 2017 with 12.6 percent, becoming the official
parliamentary opposition as the third-largest party. By the 2021 election it
retained 10.3 percent. The party has been classified by German domestic
intelligence services as a "confirmed right-wing extremist organization" in its
more radical state chapters, creating extraordinary tensions about how the
democratic system should deal with an anti-democratic party operating within
democratic institutions.

The 2021 Bundestag election produced a six-party parliament: SPD (25.7%),
CDU/CSU (24.1%), Greens (14.8%), FDP (11.5%), AfD (10.3%), Linke (4.9% but
three direct mandates). The resulting "traffic light coalition" (Ampelkoalition)
of SPD, Greens, and FDP under Olaf Scholz was Germany's most ideologically
heterogeneous governing coalition in the Federal Republic's history.

================================================================================
PART TEN: COLLAPSE OF THE SCHOLZ COALITION AND THE 2025 ELECTION
================================================================================

The Scholz coalition collapsed in November 2024 when Finance Minister Christian
Lindner (FDP) was dismissed over the budget conflict triggered by the
Constitutional Court's debt-brake ruling. The FDP left the coalition. Scholz
called a confidence vote he lost in December 2024, triggering early elections in
February 2025.

The February 2025 election was dominated by migration, economic stagnation,
energy costs, and the question of how Germany should respond to Russia's war in
Ukraine and American pressure to increase defense spending. The CDU/CSU won with
28.6 percent. The AfD came second with 20.8 percent — the highest result in the
party's history and the first time a party classified by intelligence services
as potentially extremist had come second in a federal German election. The SPD
fell to 16.4 percent, their worst result since 1887. The Greens received 11.6
percent, the FDP fell below five percent and left the Bundestag. The newly
founded BSW (Bündnis Sahra Wagenknecht) received 4.97 percent and also narrowly
missed entry.

The resulting coalition — CDU/CSU and SPD — was a new Grand Coalition under
Chancellor Friedrich Merz. Its immediate legislative priorities were large-scale
increases in defense spending and infrastructure investment, requiring
constitutional amendments to the debt brake. The Bundestag voted in March 2025
to exempt defense and security investments above one percent of GDP from the
debt brake — a historic shift in German fiscal policy requiring the necessary
two-thirds majority and passed in the outgoing parliament before new members took
their seats, itself a procedurally controversial decision.

================================================================================
PART ELEVEN: THE REICHSTAG BUILDING AS POLITICAL SYMBOL
================================================================================

No account of the Bundestag is complete without returning to the building itself.
The Reichstag was designed by Paul Wallot and completed in 1894. It bore the
inscription "Dem Deutschen Volke" (To the German People), added in 1916. It was
damaged in the 1933 fire, bombed repeatedly in the Second World War, and left
in a half-ruined state in the divided city of Berlin. Soviet soldiers famously
raised the Red Flag over it in May 1945. Artists Christo and Jeanne-Claude
wrapped the entire building in silver fabric in 1995 — an installation seen by
five million visitors in two weeks and one of the most celebrated art events of
the late 20th century — before Foster's restoration began.

The Foster renovation was completed in 1999. The glass dome, open to the public
365 days a year, receives millions of visitors annually. A mirrored cone in its
center reflects natural light into the plenary chamber below, and a sloping ramp
allows visitors to walk to the top while looking down at the parliamentary chamber.
The symbolism is explicit and deliberate: the citizen above the representative,
democracy as transparency, the people sovereign over their parliament.

================================================================================
EPILOGUE: THE BUNDESTAG IN HISTORICAL PERSPECTIVE
================================================================================

The Bundestag is, by the measure of German history, a remarkable institution.
Germany's parliamentary tradition includes a parliament with no power (the
Wilhelmine Reichstag), a parliament that destroyed itself (the Weimar Reichstag),
no parliament at all (the Nazi dictatorship), a fake parliament (the East German
Volkskammer), and finally, since 1949, a parliament that has functioned
continuously, peacefully, and with genuine democratic legitimacy.

Its design incorporates the specific lessons of every previous failure. The
constructive vote of no confidence, the five-percent threshold, the strong
Constitutional Court, the weak presidency, the federalist Bundesrat, the
parliamentary army — all of these are direct responses to identified weaknesses
in earlier democratic arrangements.

This does not make it invulnerable. The rise of the AfD, the fragmentation of the
party system, the increasing difficulty of forming stable coalitions, the debate
over how to handle a potentially anti-democratic party that operates through
democratic institutions, the debt-brake controversies that constrain legislative
autonomy — these are real challenges. The Bundestag's response to the return of
war to European soil with Russia's invasion of Ukraine in 2022 required the
institution to reckon with questions of military capacity and alliance solidarity
that the postwar constitutional order had been designed to make impossible to
answer quickly.

The Bundestag carries the weight of German history in every vote. The chamber
under the glass dome, with citizens looking down, with the inscription "Dem
Deutschen Volke" on the facade, is where Germany has tried, since 1949, to be
better than it was before. That project is ongoing. It is unfinished. It is,
as the phrase goes, the heart of democracy — and like all hearts, its continued
function cannot be taken for granted.
